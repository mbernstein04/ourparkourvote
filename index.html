What's at Stake

The city is moving to rezone and sell 267 acres of public land to private developers — without a public vote. We believe Duluthians should have the final say. That's how democracy is supposed to work.

What's Happening

The Mayor and DEDA are moving to transfer the former Lester Park Golf Course to private developers for a luxury resort, golf course, and housing — on land that belongs to all Duluthians. The city transferred the 37.5-acre Lake Nine parcel to DEDA in December 2021. The remaining 230 acres are next.

The Window

A DEDA land use study — mandated under a resolution that explicitly favors housing development and a golf course — is due July 2026. Sign before they act.

Why It Matters

Once public land is sold, it's gone forever. Duluth already has the trail builders, skiers, cyclists, and park planners to build something extraordinary here — on our own terms, without selling it. We don't need developers at the table. In fact, it's better if they're not. Learn more →

Frequently Asked Questions

What will the petitions actually do?

Petition #1 (230 acres): The former Lester Park Golf Course is currently zoned parkland. Before it can be developed, it must be rezoned. This petition requires that rezoning to go to a public vote first — giving every Duluth voter a direct say before any land changes hands. It does not block development permanently. It requires a vote.

Petition #2 (Lake Nine, 37.5 acres): The Lake Nine parcel was conveyed to DEDA in December 2021 and is currently zoned mixed-use. This petition directs the City to rezone it back to the Park and Open Space District (P-1) — and requires a majority vote of Duluth voters before it can be rezoned for private development. It recovers the parcel for public protection and gives future Duluthians a say in its future.

Why are there two petitions?

Because there are two legally distinct parcels. The 230-acre former golf course is still held by the City. The 37.5-acre Lake Nine parcel has already been conveyed to DEDA. Each parcel requires its own petition under Section 51 of the Duluth City Charter. Sign both.

How many signatures do you need?

Under Section 51 of the Duluth City Charter, thresholds are based on total ballots cast in the last general municipal election. 10% — approximately 3,664 valid signatures — places the ordinance on the November 2027 ballot. 20% — approximately 7,328 — forces the City Council to either adopt the ordinance or call a special election within about 75 days.

We are collecting 5,000 signatures per petition to account for the roughly 20–25% of signatures typically disqualified for technical reasons — illegible addresses, unsigned circulator affidavits, non-registered voters. A buffer is standard practice.

What happens after you collect enough signatures?

Completed petition sheets go to the Duluth City Clerk, who has 10 days to verify signatures and certify the threshold. At 10%, the ordinance goes to the City Council, which can adopt it directly or let it go to the November 2027 ballot. At 20%, the Council must adopt it or call a special election within about 75 days. The Council may also pass an alternative ordinance — as they did with Right to Repair — but if a majority of petitioners don't withdraw, both ordinances go to voters.

If I sign, will people know?

Petition signatures become part of the public record when submitted to the City Clerk — that is required by state law. Our Park Our Vote will not record, share, or use your signature information for any purpose other than submitting it to the City Clerk. We will not contact you based on your signature.

There's a land use study underway. Shouldn't we wait?

No. The resolution mandating the study explicitly favors housing development and a golf course as outcomes. A golf course alone requires 150–220 acres — leaving only 70–80 acres for 455 housing units, a hotel, retail, trails, and parking. See how the land actually divides up →

We're not asking anyone to prejudge the outcome. We're asking that Duluthians get to vote on it. The study is due July 2026. Sign before the Council acts.

When was Our Park Our Vote formed?

Mid-February 2026, following the City Council's passage of Ordinance 25-032-O in December 2021 authorizing the conditional transfer of the former Lester Park Golf Course to DEDA.
