What We're Doing

Our Park Our Vote is running two citizen petitions to protect all of Lester Park. The first requires a public vote before the 230-acre former golf course can be rezoned for private development. The second returns the 37.5-acre Lake Nine parcel — currently zoned mixed-use — to permanent parkland status, protecting it from development under any administration.

The Backstory

How Did We
Get Here?

The Mayor wants to bring golf back to Lester Park. The problem? The city can't afford it. But a private developer from Florida can.

The Developer's Vision

A Florida-based developer envisions a “Vail-Breckenridge” style chalet hotel on public parkland. Other courses he's developed charge up to $415 per round. This isn't community golf — it's a luxury resort built on land that belongs to Duluthians.

Why City Hall Wants the Deal

Enger Park Golf Course needs a new clubhouse, and the money from selling Lester is earmarked to build it. In other words, one public park is being sacrificed to fund improvements at another — without voters ever being asked.

Two Petitions. One Goal.

Protect All 267 Acres
of Lester Park

The Reinert administration wants to develop both the 230-acre former golf course and the 37.5-acre Lake Nine parcel. We're running two petitions to stop both — and return every acre to permanent public protection.

Petition #1: Require a Public Vote

The 230-acre former golf course cannot be transferred to private developers without first being rezoned. Our primary petition requires that rezoning to go to a public ballot — giving every Duluthian a direct vote on Lester's future before any land changes hands.

Petition #2: Return Lake Nine to Parkland

The 37.5-acre Lake Nine parcel is currently zoned mixed-use — a vulnerability the Reinert administration could exploit for development independent of the main transfer. Our companion petition rezones it back to permanent parkland status, closing that door entirely.

The Track Record Speaks for Itself

The developers behind the Lester Park proposal say they’ll include 68 units of “affordable or workforce” housing. Two things are worth understanding about that promise.

First, the math. Genuinely affordable housing — units restricted to families earning 30–60% of area median income — generates less rental revenue than market-rate units. That gap is normally covered by public tax credits, grants, or deed restrictions enforced by government agencies. Without those mechanisms, there is no legal obligation keeping any unit affordable. A developer who funds their own project privately answers to no one on affordability once the deal closes.

Second, we’ve seen this before — right here in Duluth. One of the Lester Park proposal’s own local partners, Brian Forcier, publicly announced his Ordean building downtown as “workforce housing.” It opened as 3–12 month extended-stay corporate lodging. That wasn’t a financing failure. The money was there. The promise just quietly became something else.

Meanwhile, the promises keep coming — and falling short:

  • “Workforce” housing that isn’t housing. Developers label units as “workforce housing” to sound affordable, but these often turn out to be short-term rentals — 3-to-12-month stays that function more like corporate lodging than homes for Duluth families.
  • Affordability that expires. When developers do include affordable units, the affordability requirements often have sunset clauses. After a few years, rents climb to market rate and the “affordable” housing quietly disappears.
  • Promised units that never materialize. Developers routinely pledge affordable units to win public support and approvals, then scale back or eliminate them during construction, citing rising costs or changing market conditions.
  • Public land given away, public benefit withheld. Duluth has seen developers receive public land, tax breaks, and other incentives in exchange for affordable housing commitments — only to underdeliver or walk away entirely.

Now we’re being asked to trust that a private developer from Florida will deliver 68 units of “affordable or workforce” housing on public parkland — with no binding mechanism to enforce that promise. History says otherwise.
